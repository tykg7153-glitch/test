By moving away from a flat Cartesian grid and toward a **Reference Frame Hierarchy**, you solve one of the biggest problems in mapping: "Floating Point Error" (where precision is lost the further you move from a fixed center).

### 1. Technical Feasibility: The "Scene Graph" Logic

The structure you're describing is technically a **Recursive Scene Graph** or a **Hierarchical Transformation Tree**. In this system, every node (Point, Area, or Volume) doesn't have an absolute coordinate like ; instead, it has a **Local Coordinate** relative to its parent’s center.

* **Containment via Edges:** Instead of just "connecting" points like a spiderweb, your edges define relationships. You have **Containment Edges** (e.g., *Kitchen* is inside *First Floor*) and **Adjacency Edges** (e.g., *Kitchen* is next to *Dining Room*).
* **Calculations (Distances/Angles):** To calculate the distance between two distant points (say, a desk in an office in NYC and a chair in a cafe in London), the system performs a **Least Common Ancestor (LCA) Search**. It traverses up the tree to find the first shared parent (e.g., "Earth"), converts both coordinates to that frame, and calculates the vector.

### 1. Resolving the "Overlap" Challenge

In a strict hierarchy, a node only has one parent. But in the real world, a "Point" might be in two "Areas" simultaneously (e.g., a doorway between a hall and a kitchen).

* **The DAG (Directed Acyclic Graph) Approach:** Instead of a tree, use a DAG. This allows a child node to have multiple parent nodes. A point on a boundary can belong to both `Area A` and `Area B`.
* **Containment vs. Ownership:** Distinguish between "Spatial Containment" (where it is physically) and "Functional Ownership" (who controls it). This allows your edges to describe different *types* of relationships without breaking the coordinate math.
